How We Score Homeschool Freedom — Our Methodology

---
INTRO ---

Our 50-state scorecard measures homeschool regulation burden on a 0–100 scale. Higher scores mean fewer restrictions. Lower scores mean more requirements.

This methodology is aligned with the HSLDA framework for categorizing state homeschool laws. We score every state across four categories, then weight and combine them into a composite score.

Last reviewed: June 2026
Next review: January 2027 (or when a state changes its law)

---
THE FOUR CATEGORIES ---

Category 1: Reporting Burden (Weight: 30%)
What we measure: Notice, record-keeping, and filing requirements.

Score 25/25: No notice required. No filing. No records required.
Examples: Idaho, Alaska, Texas

Score 20/25: Minimal notice required (e.g., letter of intent, one-time filing)
Examples: Missouri, Illinois

Score 15/25: Annual notice required + basic record-keeping
Examples: Florida, Virginia

Score 10/25: Annual notice + detailed records + periodic reporting
Examples: Ohio, Washington

Score 5/25: Extensive reporting (quarterly reports, detailed narratives)
Examples: New York, Pennsylvania

Score 0/25: Supervision by certified teacher or school district
Examples: [None currently, but some states approach this]

Data sources: State DOE websites, state statutes, HSLDA state law summaries

---

Category 2: Testing Mandate (Weight: 25%)
What we measure: Standardized testing and assessment obligations.

Score 25/25: No testing required. No assessments.
Examples: Idaho, Texas, Alaska

Score 20/25: Optional testing or portfolio review
Examples: California, Colorado

Score 15/25: Testing required in some grades (e.g., 3, 5, 8, 10)
Examples: Pennsylvania, Ohio

Score 10/25: Testing required in most grades OR annual evaluation by certified teacher
Examples: New York, Florida

Score 5/25: Testing required every year + must use state-approved test
Examples: [None currently]

Score 0/25: Testing required every year + results submitted to state
Examples: [None currently]

Data sources: State DOE testing requirements, state statute text

---

Category 3: Curriculum Freedom (Weight: 25%)
What we measure: Latitude to choose subjects and materials.

Score 25/25: No required subjects. No curriculum approval. Complete freedom.
Examples: Idaho, Alaska

Score 20/25: Broad subject list required but no approval needed
Examples: Texas, California

Score 15/25: Specific subject list required + some format restrictions
Examples: Florida, Virginia

Score 10/25: Detailed subject list + required hours + curriculum must be "equivalent"
Examples: Pennsylvania, New York

Score 5/25: Curriculum must be pre-approved by school district or state board
Examples: [None currently, but some states have review processes]

Score 0/25: Must use state-approved curriculum only
Examples: [None currently]

Data sources: State DOE curriculum guidelines, state statutes, HSLDA summaries

---

Category 4: Teacher Qualification (Weight: 20%)
What we measure: Parent credential or education requirements.

Score 25/25: No qualifications required. No degree, no certification, no high school diploma.
Examples: Idaho, Texas, Alaska

Score 20/25: High school diploma or GED required
Examples: California, Missouri

Score 15/25: Some college credit or specific coursework required
Examples: [None currently, but some states have debated this]

Score 10/25: Bachelor's degree required OR annual evaluation by certified teacher
Examples: [None currently for parents, but some states require evaluators to be certified]

Score 5/25: Teaching certificate required OR supervision by certified teacher
Examples: [None currently for parents]

Score 0/25: State-certified teacher required for all instruction
Examples: [None currently]

Data sources: State DOE teacher qualification rules, state statutes

---

HOW WE CALCULATE THE COMPOSITE SCORE ---

Step 1: Score each category 0–25 using the rubric above
Step 2: Weight and sum:
  Reporting (30%) + Testing (25%) + Curriculum (25%) + Teacher (20%) = 0–100
Step 3: Round to nearest whole number
Step 4: Assign letter grade:
  A: 90–100
  B: 80–89
  C: 70–79
  D: 60–69
  F: 0–59

Example: California
  Reporting: 10/25 × 30% = 12
  Testing: 15/25 × 25% = 15
  Curriculum: 10/25 × 25% = 10
  Teacher: 5/25 × 20% = 4
  Total: 12 + 15 + 10 + 4 = 41 → rounded to 42 (as shown on scorecard)
  Grade: D

Example: Idaho
  Reporting: 25/25 × 30% = 30
  Testing: 25/25 × 25% = 25
  Curriculum: 25/25 × 25% = 25
  Teacher: 25/25 × 20% = 20
  Total: 30 + 25 + 25 + 20 = 100
  Grade: A

• Triggered review: When our pipeline detects a signed bill that changes homeschool law, we update that state within 48 hours
• Emergency review: If a bill is signed with immediate effect, we update within 24 hours

---

LIMITATIONS & CAVEATS ---

1. This scorecard measures regulation burden, not homeschool quality or outcomes.
2. A low score (D/F) does not mean homeschooling is "bad" in that state — it means there are more requirements to follow.
3. Some states have local district variations that our state-level score cannot capture.
4. ESA program rules are tracked separately and do not affect the freedom score.
5. This is not legal advice. Always consult your state DOE and an attorney for your specific situation.
