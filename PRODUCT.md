# Homeschool Compass — Product Definition

> **Register:** Product (SaaS). The website is the sales funnel; the flagship is iOS/iPad native.
> **Reviewed:** 2026-06-19

## Core Purpose

An operational clearinghouse for the funded homeschool. Not a curriculum planner or calendar app — the administrative layer that sits between a homeschooling family and the state bureaucracy that funds them.

## Target Users

**Primary:** Homeschool parents in ESA/choice states managing $6K–$10K+/yr in state education funds. These are families who have chosen to homeschool but now face government compliance requirements — notification forms, quarterly assessments, instruction-day logs, portfolio audits, and provider invoices.

**Secondary:** Non-ESA families on the iPad app. The PDF compliance pack serves non-iOS prospects until app launch.

## The Fear (What Drives Purchase)

Government overreach and sudden regulation changes. The core emotional driver is:

> *"What if they change the rules and I lose what we've earned? What if I miss a deadline and my child loses their funding?"*

This translates into concrete anxieties:
- "Will I know before the laws change?"
- "Am I getting everything my child is entitled to?"
- "What if I fill out a form wrong and get audited?"
- "How do I make sure my child doesn't lack for opportunities because I missed something?"

## Value Proposition

**Armor for the funded homeschool.** Not tracking for tracking's sake — audit-proofing and entitlement defense. Know before the laws change, document everything correctly, get what your child is owed.

## Brand Personality

**Protective · Assured · Clear**

- *Protective* — You're on their side against the bureaucracy maze. The posture of a watchdog, not a door-to-door salesman.
- *Assured* — Confidence that their paperwork is correct, their deadlines are met, their funding is protected. No alarmism — calm competence.
- *Clear* — Government compliance is opaque by design. We make it legible. Plain language, clear next actions, no regulatory jargon.

## Channel Strategy

| Channel | Role |
|---------|------|
| **Landing page** (website) | Sales funnel for the iOS/iPad app. Establishes trust, demonstrates value, drives app store conversion. |
| **iOS/iPad app** | Flagship product. Native SwiftUI experience with StoreKit IAP. Ongoing portfolio management, bill tracking, compliance forms, provider invoice portal. |
| **PDF compliance pack** | Bridge product for non-iOS families (Android, desktop). One-time purchase — state-specific compliance packet with bill triage, ESA data, forms, and action items. Also serves as a low-friction entry point. |

## Key Design Principles

1. **Trust first** — The site must feel like a safe, credible authority. No aggressive upsells or overwhelming copy. The product sells itself through demonstrated competence.
2. **Simplicity over feature density** — A parent stressed about compliance doesn't want more complexity. Every screen should answer: *"What do I do next?"*
3. **Regulatory clarity** — Government forms are intentionally confusing. We translate, don't mirror. Plain language, bullet points, clear deadlines.
4. **Privacy as feature** — ZK encryption on the provider portal isn't technical overhead; it's a marketing asset. "Your provider data never touches our servers unencrypted."

## Tone & Voice

- **Warm but professional** — This is bureaucratic paperwork, but it matters to their family. Respect the gravity without being cold.
- **Reassuring, not alarmist** — The threats are real (regulations change, deadlines pass). Frame as preparation, not panic.
- **Straight talk** — No marketing fluff. If a state has a confusing form, say so. Honesty builds trust faster than polish.

## Design Principles (Extended)

Beyond the product-specific principles above, this project follows a broader design philosophy:

**Hate generic. Love intentional.** Every element earns its place. No decorative fluff, no placeholder gradients, no "this is fine" defaults. If a button is there, it has a reason. If a card is that wide, it's because the content demands it. The page should feel *chosen*, not assembled.

- No "startup SaaS" patterns that feel borrowed
- No color gradients used solely because they're trendy
- No empty states that say "nothing here yet" without guidance
- No animations that exist to demo the framework instead of the user's need
- Every visual decision has a job: reduce anxiety, surface the next action, or communicate trust

## Anti-Reference

**Do not look like:**

- Government websites (DMV, IRS, .edu portals) — we're the antidote to their opacity
- Teal-and-white B2B SaaS (HubSpot clones, "enterprise-grade" cookie-cutter)
- Bro-y startup landing pages (exploding head animations, "disrupt the classroom!")
- Curriculum/planner apps (Google Classroom, Canvas, Kahn Academy clones)
- Anything that could be mistaken for a template

**Do look like:**

- A well-edited monograph or print document — intentional white space, restrained palette, the sense that a human designed this for other humans
- A financial dashboard meets a field guide — data-dense but navigable, authoritative without being cold
- Something your child's caseworker would take seriously if printed and handed to them

## What We Are Not

- Not a curriculum provider
- Not a social network for homeschoolers
- Not an academic planner / gradebook
- Not competing with Khan Academy, Outschool, or co-op finders

Our lane: the paperwork layer between the family and the state treasury.
